Asthma Attack Action Plan: What to Do Before, During, and After

Asthma affects approximately 25 million Americans, and despite being a manageable condition, it still causes nearly 4,000 deaths annually in the United States. The majority of these deaths are preventable with proper management and a clear action plan. An asthma action plan is a written document you create with your doctor that outlines exactly what to do based on your symptom severity ‚Äî from daily maintenance to emergency response.

The Three Zones: Green, Yellow, Red

Most asthma action plans use a traffic-light system based on symptoms and peak flow readings:

  • Green Zone (Doing Well) ‚Äî No cough, wheeze, chest tightness, or shortness of breath. Peak flow is 80-100% of personal best. You can do all normal activities. Continue daily controller medications as prescribed.
  • Yellow Zone (Getting Worse) ‚Äî Coughing, wheezing, chest tightness, or waking at night due to asthma. Peak flow is 50-80% of personal best. This is your early warning ‚Äî take action now to prevent a full attack.
  • Red Zone (Medical Alert) ‚Äî Severe shortness of breath, rescue inhaler not helping, difficulty walking or talking, lips or fingernails turn blue. Peak flow below 50%. This is an emergency. Call 911.

What to Do During an Asthma Attack

When symptoms escalate beyond the green zone, act quickly:

  • Step 1: Sit upright. Don't lie down ‚Äî sitting helps open your airways. Lean slightly forward with hands on knees if needed.
  • Step 2: Use your rescue inhaler (albuterol/salbutamol). Take 2-4 puffs with a spacer if available. Wait 1 minute between puffs. A spacer improves medication delivery to the lungs by 50-60%.
  • Step 3: Breathe slowly. Pursed-lip breathing ‚Äî inhale through your nose for 4 seconds, exhale through pursed lips for 6-8 seconds ‚Äî helps keep airways open longer.
  • Step 4: Wait 15-20 minutes. If symptoms improve, continue monitoring. If no improvement, take 2-4 more puffs of rescue inhaler.
  • Step 5: If still not improving after second dose, or if symptoms are severe from the start (can't speak in full sentences, blue lips, extreme difficulty breathing), call 911 immediately.

Do NOT wait to "see if it gets better" when you're in the red zone. Asthma attacks can deteriorate rapidly, and delays in treatment increase the risk of respiratory failure.

Common Asthma Triggers

Prevention is more effective than treatment. Identifying and avoiding your personal triggers is the foundation of asthma management:

  • Allergens: Dust mites, pet dander, pollen, mold. Use allergen-proof bedding covers, HEPA filters, and keep indoor humidity below 50%.
  • Respiratory infections: Colds and flu are the #1 trigger for asthma attacks. Annual flu vaccination is recommended for all asthma patients.
  • Exercise: Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction affects up to 90% of people with asthma. Using a rescue inhaler 15 minutes before exercise prevents symptoms in most cases. Don't avoid exercise ‚Äî it improves lung function long-term.
  • Cold air: Breathing cold, dry air can trigger bronchospasm. Wear a scarf or mask over your nose and mouth in winter.
  • Air pollution and smoke: Check air quality indexes (AQI) and limit outdoor activity when levels are unhealthy. Tobacco smoke ‚Äî including secondhand ‚Äî is a potent trigger.
  • Strong emotions and stress: Laughing, crying, or anxiety can trigger symptoms through hyperventilation and vagal nerve stimulation.

Daily Controller Medications

Rescue inhalers treat acute symptoms but don't address the underlying airway inflammation. If you use your rescue inhaler more than twice a week (excluding pre-exercise use), you likely need a daily controller medication:

  • Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS): The cornerstone of asthma treatment. Fluticasone (Flovent), budesonide (Pulmicort), and beclomethasone reduce airway inflammation and prevent attacks. They're not the same as systemic steroids ‚Äî inhaled doses are tiny and mostly stay in the lungs.
  • Combination inhalers (ICS/LABA): Fluticasone/salmeterol (Advair), budesonide/formoterol (Symbicort). Add a long-acting bronchodilator to the steroid for moderate-to-severe asthma.
  • Biologics: For severe, uncontrolled asthma, injectable biologics (omalizumab, dupilumab, mepolizumab) target specific immune pathways. They're expensive but can be life-changing for the right patients.

Peak Flow Monitoring

A peak flow meter is an inexpensive device that measures how fast you can blow air out of your lungs. Regular monitoring (especially in the morning before medications) provides objective data that catches worsening asthma before symptoms become obvious.

To establish your personal best: measure peak flow twice daily for 2-3 weeks when your asthma is well-controlled. Your personal best is the highest number achieved. All zone boundaries in your action plan are percentages of this number.

Creating Your Action Plan

Work with your doctor to create a written plan that includes: your personal best peak flow, specific medications and doses for each zone, emergency contact numbers, and when to seek emergency care. The NHLBI provides free templates you can fill out with your provider. Keep copies at home, work, school, and in your phone.

Review and update your plan at least annually, or sooner if your asthma control changes. A good action plan empowers you to manage most flares at home and know exactly when professional help is needed.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my asthma is well-controlled?

Well-controlled asthma means: daytime symptoms no more than twice a week, no nighttime awakenings, rescue inhaler use no more than twice a week, no limitations on normal activities, and peak flow above 80% of personal best. If you're not meeting these criteria, your treatment needs adjustment.

Can adults develop asthma for the first time?

Yes. Adult-onset asthma is common, particularly in women, and can be triggered by respiratory infections, hormonal changes, occupational exposures, or new allergies. It tends to be more persistent than childhood asthma and often requires daily controller medication.

Is it safe to exercise with asthma?

Absolutely. Regular exercise improves cardiovascular fitness and lung capacity. Use a rescue inhaler 15 minutes before exercise if needed, warm up gradually, and avoid exercising in very cold or high-pollution conditions. Swimming is particularly well-tolerated due to the warm, humid air.
