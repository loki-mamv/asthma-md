Types of Asthma Inhalers: A Complete Guide

February 9, 2026 · 10 min read
Treatment Inhalers

Inhalers remain the primary delivery method for asthma medication because they deposit drugs directly into the airways, maximizing therapeutic effect while minimizing systemic side effects. Yet the number of inhaler devices and drug combinations available today can be overwhelming. Understanding what each type does and when to use it is essential for effective asthma control.

According to the NHLBI Expert Panel Report, asthma medications fall into two broad categories: quick-relief (rescue) medications that treat acute symptoms, and long-term controller medications that reduce airway inflammation over time. Some newer devices combine both functions. Choosing the right inhaler depends on asthma severity, the patient's ability to use the device correctly, and individual treatment goals.

Rescue Inhalers: Short-Acting Beta-Agonists

Short-acting beta-agonists (SABAs) are the first-line treatment for acute asthma symptoms. Albuterol (known as salbutamol outside the United States) is the most widely prescribed SABA worldwide. These medications work by relaxing the smooth muscle bands that wrap around the airways, opening them within minutes. Relief typically lasts four to six hours.

Every asthma patient should have a rescue inhaler accessible at all times. However, frequent use is a warning sign. The GINA guidelines state that needing a SABA more than twice per week (outside of pre-exercise use) suggests inadequate asthma control and the need for a step-up in controller therapy. Relying heavily on rescue inhalers without addressing the underlying inflammation is one of the most common management errors clinicians encounter.

Common SABAs include albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin, Proventil) and levalbuterol (Xopenex). Side effects are generally mild and include transient tremor, increased heart rate, and jitteriness. These effects typically diminish with regular use.

Controller Inhalers: Inhaled Corticosteroids

Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) are the most effective long-term controller medications for persistent asthma. They work by suppressing the chronic airway inflammation that drives asthma symptoms. Unlike oral corticosteroids, inhaled formulations deliver medication directly to the lungs, greatly reducing the risk of systemic side effects such as bone thinning, weight gain, and immune suppression.

ICS do not provide immediate relief. Their anti-inflammatory effects build over days to weeks of consistent use. This is a critical point: many patients stop taking their controller inhaler when they feel well, which leads to a gradual return of airway inflammation and eventual flare-ups. The NHLBI emphasizes that controller medications must be taken daily as prescribed, even during symptom-free periods.

Commonly prescribed ICS include:

  • Fluticasone propionate (Flovent) — available in low, medium, and high doses
  • Budesonide (Pulmicort) — also available as a nebulizer suspension for young children
  • Beclomethasone (QVAR) — uses a fine-particle formulation for improved lung deposition
  • Mometasone (Asmanex) — once-daily dosing option for some patients
  • Ciclesonide (Alvesco) — a prodrug activated in the lungs, which may reduce oral side effects

Local side effects of ICS include oral thrush (candidiasis) and hoarseness. Using a spacer with metered-dose inhalers and rinsing the mouth after each use effectively prevents both problems.

Combination Inhalers

For patients whose asthma is not adequately controlled on ICS alone, combination inhalers pair an inhaled corticosteroid with a long-acting beta-agonist (LABA). LABAs keep the airways open for 12 hours or more but should never be used without an accompanying corticosteroid due to safety concerns identified in large clinical trials. Combination devices solve this problem by packaging both medications together.

Widely used combination inhalers include:

  • Fluticasone/salmeterol (Advair) — available as a diskus dry powder inhaler and an MDI
  • Budesonide/formoterol (Symbicort) — also approved for as-needed use in some treatment approaches
  • Mometasone/formoterol (Dulera)
  • Fluticasone furoate/vilanterol (Breo Ellipta) — once-daily dosing

The 2023 GINA report introduced a notable shift in treatment philosophy: recommending ICS-formoterol combinations as both the maintenance and reliever therapy (known as MART or SMART therapy) for many patients. This approach uses budesonide/formoterol for both daily control and symptom relief, replacing the traditional separate rescue inhaler. Clinical trials have shown that MART reduces severe exacerbations compared with conventional fixed-dose ICS-LABA plus as-needed SABA.

Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonists

Long-acting muscarinic antagonists (LAMAs), originally developed for COPD, have gained a role in asthma management for patients not well controlled on ICS-LABA combinations. Tiotropium (Spiriva Respimat) is the most studied LAMA in asthma and is approved as add-on therapy for patients aged six and older with moderate to severe persistent asthma.

LAMAs work differently from beta-agonists. Instead of directly relaxing airway smooth muscle, they block acetylcholine receptors, reducing the parasympathetic bronchoconstriction that contributes to airway narrowing. Adding tiotropium to ICS-LABA therapy has been shown to improve lung function and reduce exacerbation rates in several large randomized trials.

Inhaler Devices: MDIs, DPIs, and SMIs

The medication inside the inhaler matters, but so does the device that delivers it. Poor inhaler technique is remarkably common. A systematic review published in the journal Respiratory Medicine found that 70 to 80 percent of patients make at least one critical error when using their inhaler, significantly reducing the amount of medication that reaches the lungs.

Metered-Dose Inhalers (MDIs)

MDIs use a pressurized canister to deliver a measured dose of medication as a fine aerosol mist. They require coordination between pressing the canister and breathing in, which many patients find difficult. Using a valved holding chamber (spacer) eliminates the need for precise timing and increases drug delivery to the lungs by roughly 40 to 60 percent, according to studies in the Journal of Aerosol Medicine.

Dry Powder Inhalers (DPIs)

DPIs deliver medication as a dry powder that is inhaled through a breath-activated mechanism. Because they do not require hand-breath coordination, some patients find them easier to use. However, DPIs require a fast, forceful inhalation to disaggregate the powder, which can be challenging for young children, elderly patients, or anyone experiencing severe bronchoconstriction during an acute attack.

Soft Mist Inhalers (SMIs)

The Respimat device produces a slow-moving, long-lasting aerosol cloud that does not require the forceful inhalation of a DPI or the precise coordination of an MDI. It delivers more medication to the lungs compared to a standard MDI without a spacer, making it a good option for patients who struggle with other devices.

Nebulizers

Nebulizers convert liquid medication into a fine mist that is inhaled through a mouthpiece or face mask over five to 15 minutes. They require no coordination or special breathing technique, which makes them suitable for very young children, elderly patients, and those experiencing severe exacerbations. However, nebulizers are bulky, require a power source, and treatments take considerably longer than inhaler puffs.

For most patients, a properly used MDI with spacer delivers equivalent medication to a nebulizer. The American College of Emergency Physicians' clinical guidelines note that MDIs with spacers are as effective as nebulizers for delivering bronchodilators in acute asthma in the emergency department.

Getting Your Technique Right

Regardless of the device, proper technique is non-negotiable. Ask your pharmacist or respiratory therapist to demonstrate your specific inhaler and watch you use it. Technique should be re-checked at every clinic visit, as errors creep back in over time. The steps vary by device, but general principles apply: exhale fully before inhaling the medication, breathe in slowly and deeply (for MDIs) or quickly and forcefully (for DPIs), hold your breath for about 10 seconds after inhalation, and rinse your mouth if using an ICS.

If you are prescribed multiple inhalers, always use your rescue inhaler first when you are symptomatic, as opening the airways improves delivery of subsequent medications.
